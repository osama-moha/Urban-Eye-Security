Airbnb & Short-Stay Security

Protecting short-stay rentals without compromising guest privacy

A practical guide to controlled access, exterior camera placement, life-safety sensors, and protecting vacant periods without making guests feel watched.

Urban Eye Security May 2026 7 min read
Back to Insights

A short-stay property has two security responsibilities at the same time: protect the owner’s asset and respect the guest’s private space. The right plan does not depend on intrusive surveillance. It controls who enters, protects life-safety risks, documents legitimate access, and secures the unit when it is unoccupied.

Start with privacy before choosing equipment

A guest should be able to stay in a rental without being recorded inside the home. Airbnb prohibits security cameras and recording devices that monitor any interior part of a home, even when the device is turned off or disconnected. Exterior recording devices may be used only within the platform rules and must be disclosed in the listing.

For a Kenyan short-stay operator, camera footage may also involve personal data. Before installing cameras, decide why each viewing area is necessary, who can access the footage, how long it will be retained, and how guests will be informed. A security design should be capable of being explained clearly to a guest before they book.

The objective is not to observe guests. It is to control access, deter avoidable risk, and create a clear response path when the property is threatened.

Replace permanent keys with controlled access

Traditional keys are difficult to manage across multiple guest stays, cleaners, maintenance visits, and late check-outs. Once a key is copied or not returned, it becomes difficult to know who can still access the property.

A smart lock or managed keypad can provide a separate temporary access code for each stay. The code can be scheduled to begin at check-in and expire after check-out. Cleaners and maintenance providers can receive their own restricted access windows rather than sharing a single permanent key or code.

The value is accountability: access can be managed around real bookings and approved work visits, while old guest access is removed when the stay ends.

Use exterior visibility only where it is justified

Outdoor camera coverage can help protect arrival points and shared asset areas such as a front gate, driveway, parking bay, exterior entrance, or building access corridor under the host’s control. Camera placement should be limited to security-relevant areas and should never point into private indoor spaces or secluded outdoor areas where guests expect privacy.

All exterior security cameras or recording devices under the host’s control should be disclosed in the listing with their location described accurately. This is not merely a courtesy: it helps guests make an informed booking decision and avoids a security feature becoming a trust problem.

Prioritise life safety and property-damage prevention

The least intrusive technology in a short-stay property is often the most valuable. Working smoke alarms help protect guests and the building from fire risk. Carbon monoxide alarms should be considered where the unit has fuel-burning appliances, a fireplace, a generator risk, or an attached garage. Airbnb encourages hosts to install smoke and carbon monoxide alarms and keep the safety-device information in their listing current.

Water-leak sensors can also protect the property without recording guest activity. Sensors near sinks, washing machines, water heaters, tanks, or utility areas can provide early warning of flooding or plumbing failure during an occupied stay or while the unit is vacant.

Protect the unit between bookings

The risk profile changes when a guest checks out. A vacant unit may be exposed to forced entry, unauthorised access, incomplete lock-up after cleaning, or damage that is not discovered until the next arrival.

Door contacts, indoor intrusion detection, and alarm alerting can be used to protect a confirmed vacant period. The operating rule should be simple: interior intrusion protection is for the empty property, not for monitoring a guest while they occupy the home. Arming and disarming should also account for approved cleaners or maintenance visits.

Keep access and digital accounts clean between stays

Security is weakened when previous guests retain access after check-out. Temporary door codes should expire, any guest Wi-Fi access arrangement should be managed deliberately, and smart device permissions should be reviewed after each stay or on a defined schedule.

Smart televisions and streaming services should use guest-ready settings or dedicated hosting accounts rather than the owner’s personal profiles. Cleaners, maintenance personnel, and property managers should have individual access methods wherever possible, so one shared code does not circulate indefinitely.

What to confirm before installing short-stay security

A security proposal for a short-stay property should explain privacy boundaries as clearly as it explains equipment. Before accepting a recommendation, confirm the following points.

01

Which risks are being addressed: guest access, vacancy, fire, flooding, parking, or exterior entry?

02

Are any cameras limited to permitted exterior areas and accurately disclosed in the listing?

03

How will guest, cleaner, and maintenance access codes be issued, expired, and reviewed?

04

Which smoke, carbon monoxide, water-leak, or other life-safety risks apply to this property?

05

How is the vacant unit protected between bookings without monitoring occupied private spaces?

06

Who can access footage or event records, and how will privacy and data-handling responsibilities be managed?

Reference note

This guide should be read alongside the current Airbnb rules on cameras and recording devices, Airbnb guidance on smoke and carbon monoxide alarms, and applicable Kenyan privacy and data protection requirements overseen by the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner. Platform policies and legal obligations may change; confirm current requirements before installing or operating surveillance equipment.
